# Gordon Solie Commentary Analysis

## Presentation
Solie first and foremost displays professionalism, politeness, and respect in his vocal delivery to his audience, as well as his interviewees—regardless if they were faces or heels.

He was always dressed in a tailor-fitted suit and tie, buttoned jacket, matching suit pants, and dress shoes. Hair combed to the side, large black-rimmed glasses.  

This created an aura of professionalism dropped in the middle of the chaotic, drama-filled wrestling universe. His visual presence was quickly matched with his vocal ability to paint a detailed picture with his delivery.

## Interview Style
Solie occasionally addressed interviewees as Mr. (last name) or Sir—as if it were Gordon's talk show and he was bringing on wrestlers to express their views.  

Similar to how a respected journalist would interview important people in business or politics. This brought immediate authenticity to the program.

Despite small TV studio tapings (100–200 people), low-grade TV production, visible electronic cables sprawling across warehouse-style cement floors, background props that didn’t extend all the way (sometimes exposing walls or unfinished areas), Solie’s delivery countered the production setbacks with visual and vocal prowess.

At moments, Solie would inject a brief "uh" before speaking. I interpreted this as a moment of collection before the next wind-up of phrases.  

He was an odd and unique blend between unassuming everyday man and master communicator.

## Match Narration
Descriptive and detail-fused with anatomical depth merged with high diction. Output was a clear, easy-to-understand, steady-flowing, monotone, measured delivery.

He kept this low-voiced precision consistent, regardless of opponent styles or fan preferences when describing the action—as if he were reading aloud a transcript into a radio broadcast.

The exceptions to the robotic consistency were hand-picked moments by Solie: escalating his tone and cadence from low to mid, measured to increased pace.

His voice mirrored the action like a heartbeat mirrors physical activity. Solie pumped blood into the audio as needed, while still able to keep the same descriptive delivery—keeping the auditory painting alive.

When the action would slow, it was Solie’s cue to return to his usual calm demeanored delivery.

This was the brilliance of Solie: to save his excitement for calculated moments, as if he were cataloging key moments for the fans. Solie was indeed the dean of commentary, as many have noted.
